From Bitcoin to Distributed Ledgers: A New Era of Shared Trust

Key transitions

  • Bitcoin (2008) introduced the first decentralised ledger, verified by peers instead of central authorities.
  • The model prompted a vigorous debate between those championing democratised ownership and critics wary of speculation and illicit activity.
  • The outcome was an explosion of Distributed Ledger Technologies (DLTs) — including blockchains, DAGs, and hybrid architectures.
“DLT removes the greedy middle that manages and exploits our assets and identities for financial reward.” Ferraro et al.

Why Shared Resources Need Smarter Compliance

Narrative

  • The economy is shifting from consumers to prosumers who both use and contribute to services.
  • Shared systems thrive on trust and cooperation across human and machine actors.
  • The challenge: encouraging responsible behaviour without heavy central control.
  • Traditional enforcement—laws, ratings, manual oversight—is sluggish for dense IoT environments.

Smart City Problems of Shared Behaviour

Dynamic deposits link behaviour to access, helping cities protect shared infrastructure and reduce misuse.

How to Incentivise Social Compliance in Smart Cities?

Key points

  • Systems rarely fail for lack of technology; they fail when behaviour misaligns with collective goals.
  • DLT can make enforcement transparent, scalable, and auditable across city-scale networks.
  • Digital deposits and rebates ensure compliance is the economically rational option.

Limitations of Blockchain and the Move to DAGs

Blockchain limitations

  • ⛏️ Proof-of-Work consumes vast energy.
  • ⏳ Throughput averages only 7–20 transactions per second.
  • 💰 Transaction fees prevent fine-grained micro-trades.
  • ⚖️ Competitive consensus drifts toward centralisation.

DAG advantages

  • Cooperative validation keeps latency low and fees at zero.
  • Supports dense IoT ecosystems and machine-to-machine microtransactions.

Each new transaction approves two previous ones, propagating consensus throughout the tangle.

The Logic of the Tangle: Cooperative Consensus

Flow of validation

  • Every new transaction approves two earlier transactions.
  • Validated transactions accumulate “weight,” while unapproved ones remain as “tips.”
  • Randomised tip selection produces a directed acyclic graph.
  • Continuous approval cycles stabilise the network over time.

A lightweight proof-of-work discourages spam and prevents double spending.

DLT as a Mechanism for Social Compliance

Concept summary

  • Traditional pricing: pay for access, regardless of behaviour.
  • Dynamic deposit pricing: stake tokens that are refunded when behaviour remains responsible.
  • Deposits are forfeited when rules are broken; falling below a threshold escalates to authorities.

Traffic example

  • Drivers receive a finite token balance along with their licence.
  • Compliant driving preserves the balance.
  • Violations deduct tokens, and repeated offences trigger intervention.

Why DAGs excel

  • High transaction frequency sustains ledger stability.
  • Zero fees support ubiquitous machine-to-machine payments.
  • Tamper resistance preserves auditability across agencies.
