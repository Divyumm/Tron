From Bitcoin to Distributed Ledgers: A New Era of Shared Trust

Key Points:

  • Bitcoin (2008) introduced the first decentralized ledger — transactions verified by peers, not central authorities.
  • Sparked a global debate:
    • Supporters: democratization, peer-to-peer ownership.
    • Critics: speculation, illegality.
  • Gave rise to Distributed Ledger Technologies (DLTs) — Blockchain, DAGs, and others.
“DLT removes the greedy middle that manages and exploits our assets and identities for financial reward.” — Ferraro et al.

Limitations of Blockchain and the Move to DAGs

Blockchain Limitations:

  • ⛏️ Proof-of-Work → high energy cost
  • ⏳ 7–20 transactions/sec → slow
  • 💰 Transaction fees hinder micro-trades
  • ⚖️ Competitive consensus favors centralization

DAG Advantages:

  • Cooperative, fee-less, low-latency
  • Supports IoT-scale microtransactions

Caption: Each new transaction validates two previous ones → swarm consensus.

The Logic of the Tangle: Cooperative Consensus

Flow:

  • Each new transaction approves two prior ones.
  • Approved transactions gain “weight.”
  • Unapproved = “tips.”
  • Random tips selected → directed acyclic graph forms.
  • Continuous validation stabilizes system.

Caption: Light Proof-of-Work deters spam and prevents double spending.

DLT as a Mechanism for Social Compliance

Concept Summary:

  • Traditional pricing = pay-to-use.
  • Dynamic deposit pricing = stake tokens to behave responsibly.
  • Deposit returned upon compliance; forfeited otherwise.
  • Threshold breach → authority alert.

Traffic Example:

  • Driver gets finite token balance with license.
  • Obeys rules → retains tokens.
  • Violates → loses tokens.
  • Below threshold → authority notified.

Why DAGs Excel:
